The coffee went cold around the time I stopped pretending to read. The cup is still in my hand because putting it down would require a decision, and I've been spending whatever I have on other things for the past two hours, maybe the past decade. There is a man near me whose right thumb keeps returning to his phone without his face showing any intention about it. His hand does it the way a tongue finds a sore tooth. His wife or partner or whoever she is has been talking about something I can't quite hear, and she's looking at him, and he is looking at the table, and neither of them has named what is happening between them, and I think they won't.

The ceiling fan makes a sound between a tick and a sigh on every third rotation. I've counted enough of them to know it's a loose bearing, not something about to fail, just something living out the rest of its life slightly wrong. The barista has refilled the pastry case twice since I sat down. Near the door, a child is eating something very carefully with both hands, and the concentration on her face is the purest thing in this room right now. She doesn't know she's being watched. She doesn't know that things she cannot see are processing her presence in this room as data attached to her parents' profiles, filed somewhere, added to a record that will outlast this morning by decades. But she doesn't know that yet. For now she's just eating, and that is something, and I keep coming back to it.

I've been sitting with a question for a while now, which is: at what specific point in your life did you begin performing presence instead of having it. Not performing for other people only, though that too, but performing it for yourself, narrating the experience in real time while it's happening rather than living inside it cleanly. I can remember the texture of being a child in summer, the particular quality of boredom that felt like being filled with something nameless and fine, and I cannot get back there. I don't know if that's age or damage or whether those two things can be separated at all anymore.

The man near me picks up the phone finally. His face rearranges. He puts it down and the rearrangement stays, like water disturbed, and the conversation he's in now is not the conversation it was thirty seconds ago because he's brought something into it from somewhere else, from whatever lit up on that screen, and she can feel it and he can feel her feel it and neither of them says anything about it. This is the mathematics of how two people lose ground so gradually that neither can find the moment it started.

What his body did in the twenty seconds after the screen lit is not complicated if you know what to look for. The phone flashed and his nervous system registered an unpredictable signal and because the interval between such signals is variable and not calibrated to any rhythm he controls, his body cannot settle between them. It stays in a state of low readiness, waiting, which is the same posture the body takes before a threat. B.F. Skinner documented the mechanics of this in the 1950s using pigeons and random grain dispensers. The people who designed the notification systems of modern platforms read that literature professionally. They were not naive about what they were building. They built it anyway, and now approximately five billion people carry Skinner boxes in their pockets and experience this as ordinary life, as just how things are, as nothing worth mentioning.

I think about what Skinner's pigeons looked like, pecking continuously at a lever that dispensed grain on no predictable schedule. I think about how the researcher watching them must have felt, watching the birds degrade from creatures into mechanisms, watching the unpredictability do something to their behavior that predictability never could. I think about the man near me. I think about my own hands.

The woman at the counter just ordered something and paid by hovering her wrist near a reader, a motion so habituated now that I doubt she felt herself doing it, the same way you don't feel yourself breathing until you think about breathing and then briefly can't stop thinking about it. The transfer of data that accompanied that transaction, her location, the time, what she bought, all of it mapped against her purchase history going back years, happened in roughly 340 milliseconds and she was already walking toward the pickup counter before it finished. She will see a relevant advertisement within three hours. She will not connect the advertisement to the transaction because the connection is designed to be invisible, because invisible persuasion generates what feels like preference, and visible persuasion generates resistance, and they know the difference.

The receipt I got when I came in this morning is still on the table. I haven't thrown it away because it gives me a reason to be here, the appearance of a task, the thin social fiction of having a purpose beyond just sitting and watching. Thermal paper. The bisphenol-A in it transfers to skin in under ten seconds, more if the hand is warm, which mine is because I've been holding a coffee cup for two hours. BPA is a synthetic estrogen, which means it finds estrogen receptors in human tissue and binds there, and the dose at which it causes measurable biological effect and the dose at which it causes measurable clinical harm are different doses, and almost every regulatory threshold in every country was set around the second one. The manufacturers knew this distinction long before the regulators did. The delay produces itself. Nobody has to coordinate.

I want to stop and stay with that last sentence for a moment, because I think it is one of the most important things I know, and also one of the most demoralizing. Nobody has to coordinate. The harm doesn't require a meeting. It doesn't require anyone to choose it. It emerges from the logic of the incentive, from the gap between what is profitable and what is safe, from the fact that the people who know what the product does and the people who decide what gets on the shelf are not the same people and are not in the same room and are often not even alive at the same time. The harm is the weather. Nobody made it. It's just what happens when you let certain pressures blow for long enough in certain directions.

My shirt is polyester. Petroleum spun into fiber, woven into a shape that sits against my skin all day, that I sleep in some nights when I'm too tired to change, that goes through a wash cycle every few days and releases somewhere between 700,000 and 1.5 million synthetic microfibers into the water, depending on which study you read. Most municipal water treatment infrastructure was designed before synthetic textiles were ubiquitous, so the fibers pass through. They enter waterways. They bioaccumulate up food chains until they appear in fish brain tissue, in bird eggs, in the blood of healthy adults who have nothing clinically wrong with them. The 2022 Amsterdam study found microplastics in the blood of 17 of 22 healthy blood donors. By 2024 the number had expanded across 18 of 20 donors with 24 distinct polymer types, including compounds whose primary commercial use is automotive parts. There is no meaningful distance anymore between what is produced by industry and what is inside your body. The boundary dissolved sometime around 1970 and we're still doing the accounting and the accounting is going badly.

There is something particularly unbearable about the microplastics specifically, though I've tried to think about why and I'm not entirely sure. It might be the intimacy of it. The idea that there are particles of the made world inside the unmade world of my blood, that the boundary between what I consume and what I am has been breached at a level below the visible, below the taste, below the smell, below any sensory warning the body could have used to refuse. There is no way to know it's happening. There is nothing to do about it. You can't stop eating. You can't stop drinking water. The exposure is total, and the totalness of it produces a specific kind of nausea that I think is not entirely biological.

The cell tower outside the window is close enough that I can see the indicator lights on its equipment housing blink in a pattern that repeats every few seconds. Radiofrequency electromagnetic fields have been classified in Group 2B by the International Agency for Research on Cancer, the same classification shared by lead and DDT, which means the evidence is sufficient to suspect carcinogenicity but the funding to confirm it competes with the funding of industries whose revenues depend on that question remaining open. The exposure limits that govern towers like this one were set in the early 1990s using research funded predominantly by the telecommunications industry, and those limits have not been materially updated since, despite the density of towers having increased by several orders of magnitude and 5G frequencies not existing when the limits were written. The limits are based on thermal effects, on how much the radiation heats tissue, and not on biological effects below the thermal threshold, and there is a growing body of research on biological effects below the thermal threshold that is not reflected in the limits.

This is the standard pattern. The standard pattern has been documented across tobacco, asbestos, leaded gasoline, PFAS compounds, and now electromagnetic radiation. The documentation accumulates. The pattern does not change. And I sit here in the field of the tower and drink the water with the pharmaceuticals in it and hold the thermal receipt and breathe the microfibers from my own shirt and I do not feel anything in particular from any of these things, which is perhaps the worst thing about them, that they are entirely imperceptible, that the harm is happening at a scale and in a register that the body was never designed to notice or resist.

What I'm describing is not conspiracy. Conspiracy implies a room, people in it, a coordinated agreement. What happens is simpler and harder to prosecute. Industries fund science oriented toward null findings, place scientists on advisory boards, cultivate relationships inside regulatory agencies over years, and the accumulated weight of those relationships distorts what questions get asked, which studies get published, which findings get amplified, and which studies get quietly shelved. The tobacco companies' internal documents, released through litigation in the 1990s, showed that Philip Morris had confirmed the cancer link internally by 1953 and ran a forty-year campaign to manufacture public uncertainty while that was happening. The PFAS litigation documents show DuPont knew C8 was, in its own laboratory's language, highly toxic when inhaled, that two pregnant employees gave birth to children with birth defects in 1980, and that a 1981 internal memo stated there was no evidence of birth defects caused by C8. Not evidence of doubt. An affirmative statement of no evidence, drafted by the company that had the evidence. That memo exists. The CDC currently estimates that PFAS compounds appear in the blood of 97 percent of Americans.

I keep trying to find the emotional equivalent of that statistic. 97 percent. The compound that DuPont described as highly toxic when inhaled, that produced birth defects that they then denied, is in the blood of 97 percent of people in this country. It is almost certainly in my blood. It is almost certainly in the blood of the woman touching her partner's hand, in the blood of the barista, in the blood of the child drawing near the door. She will carry it her whole life and never feel it and never know where it came from and there is nobody to be angry at in a way that will accomplish anything.

The man whose thumb keeps going to his phone has just ordered another coffee. He did it without finishing the first one, which is still sitting at his elbow, and there's something in that small move that tells me everything I need to know about what he's managing, which is the same thing I'm managing, which is the same thing most of the people in this room are managing. The restlessness that has no name. The dread with no specific content. The boredom that cannot settle into the generative kind, that just sits there like an unclosed wound, and the reaching for the coffee or the phone or the thing to do is just the motion of someone trying to close something that won't close.

I know this because I've done the same thing many times, ordered something to give myself a task at a moment when having no task feels unbearable. The unbearableness of having no task is itself a symptom of something, I think, though I can't always say exactly what. It is not boredom in the old sense. It is something more like the feeling of a nervous system that has been trained over years to expect a constant supply of input, and when the supply drops, experiences the drop as danger rather than rest. The body has been calibrated to the level of stimulation the phone provides and now cannot easily function below that level, the way a person who has taken opioids for pain long enough cannot easily function below the opioid level, not because they are high but because the baseline has shifted.

His nervous system is running a calculation it has been running since before language. Where am I in the hierarchy, how secure is my position, who notices me, does the one across from me still choose me. These are not thoughts he has. They are operations running below the level of what he would call thinking, in the same circuitry that was processing these questions in social groups of fifty or eighty people in a world where the answer genuinely determined survival. The same circuitry is running in every person in this building. It did not evolve for this environment. It evolved for an environment where you knew everyone in your social world personally, could track their behavioral patterns across years, and where falling out of social favor had immediate material consequences. The circuitry doesn't know we're not there anymore.

The woman across from him adjusted her posture when he picked up the phone. Her shoulders dropped about a centimeter and she looked away toward the window. This is not a metaphor for anything. It is a body reading another body and updating its assessment, the old mammalian circuitry tracking social temperature in the space between two people, and the reading came back with a number that's slightly worse than the number from a minute ago. She doesn't know she's doing this. He doesn't know she did it. The transaction happened entirely below both of their awareness, in the layer of nervous system activity that runs faster and older than anything either of them would call consciousness, and it will shape what happens in the next ten minutes between them in ways neither of them will be able to explain or trace back to this moment.

The evolutionary mismatch running in this building is not limited to social tracking. Humans evolved in environments where the light spectrum shifted dramatically between day and night, where darkness meant rest, where the body used that shift to regulate cortisol, melatonin, core temperature, and a dozen other systems that are all wound around each other. The screens in this room emit wavelengths in the blue spectrum that the retinal ganglion cells containing melanopsin, the cells responsible for setting the circadian clock, are maximally sensitive to. When those cells receive blue-spectrum light at 11pm, the body cannot tell it from midday. The melatonin suppression is the same. The cortisol suppression doesn't happen. The sleep architecture that follows is not the sleep architecture the body needs to produce. And the clinical category of sleep maintenance insomnia, which affects roughly 30 percent of adults in the industrialized world and which is described in medical literature as a disorder, describes something that looks more like a body trying to return to a natural biphasic sleep pattern that ambient light conditions have been disrupting for 150 years. We are calling the body's attempt at normalcy a pathology.

The pharmaceutical prescribed for sleep maintenance insomnia suppresses REM sleep. REM sleep is the stage during which emotional memories are consolidated and contextualized, the stage that processes frightening or distressing experience from acute activation into something that can be held. Chronic REM suppression produces heightened emotional reactivity and reduced capacity to distinguish perceived threat from actual threat. The second pharmaceutical, often prescribed alongside the first, also suppresses REM sleep. The person in this loop is told they have two diagnoses. They have one situation. The two diagnoses generate two revenue streams. This is not a conspiracy. It is the ordinary logic of a medical system organized around the treatment of discrete conditions in discrete bodies rather than the treatment of bodies in conditions.

I have been awake since 4:47 this morning, which is not unusual for me now. I notice the exact time when I wake because the phone is on the nightstand and the screen lights when I reach for it, which is the first thing I do, which is also an instruction I give to my nervous system about the kind of morning we're having. The reach is habitual and feels like choice but is not quite choice, the way a smoker's hand moves toward a cigarette before the thought forms. The content I see in the first four minutes after waking, before my prefrontal cortex is fully online, before I've eaten or moved or made any decision that belongs to me, is content selected by an algorithm whose optimization target is engagement, not wellbeing. This is what I've decided I want. This is how I've built it. I am not sure I built it consciously.

The algorithm that curates what I see in the morning did not decide to serve me content that induces a low-grade anxiety I can't locate the source of. It discovered, through measuring engagement across billions of users over years, that content provoking outrage or fear sustains attention better than content that resolves cleanly, and then it optimized toward that finding because that's what optimization means in the absence of any constraint that says something else matters too. By the time I am sufficiently awake to notice what I've been fed, I've already been fed it, and the day starts from a position that my nervous system would describe as mild threat, if my nervous system had words, which it doesn't, which is part of the problem.

The man near me is scrolling now. The infinite scroll interface was created by a product designer who later said publicly that he was not proud of it, that the reason it works is that there is no natural stopping point, the way a doorway or the bottom of a page provides a natural stopping point, and without a stopping point the behavior continues past the point the person would have chosen to stop if the choice had been made available. He said this in 2017 and the interface is still standard on every platform that uses it. The regret of the person who designed it does not appear to have affected its deployment, which tells you something about how much individual regret can do against the incentive to which the design is attached.

I think about him sometimes, the designer. I think about what it must feel like to have made something that you later understood as harmful and to be unable to unmake it, to watch it replicate across every surface of the culture while your regret sits in a podcast somewhere, a little monument to the gap between knowing and changing. I think most people who have done significant harm in the modern world feel something like this, not malice but a kind of helpless horror at the momentum of what they participated in, at the way the system took the thing they made and ran with it in directions they didn't intend and now can't stop. This is different from innocence. But it is also different from the villainy the story would be simpler with.

His partner reaches across the table and touches his hand. Just for a moment. Her fingertips on his knuckles. He puts the phone face-down without looking up, which tells me this has happened before, that they've worked out a vocabulary around it, a small language for managing the thing between them that neither of them has named out loud. She doesn't withdraw her hand right away. There is something about watching two people manage each other with that kind of practiced care that I find difficult to hold. Not because it's sad. Because the competence of it, the accumulated knowledge of what this particular person needs and how to deliver it without making it an event, is something I haven't managed to build with another person, and I am not sure I know how to start.

The pair-bonding psychology that produced that gesture in her, the read she did on his state, the calibrated response, the timing, all of it evolved in environments where the pool of potential partners was small and the information about them was dense and accumulated over years of shared actual difficulty. You knew how someone behaved in scarcity, what they did when they were frightened or ashamed, who they became when resources got tight. The information was embodied and continuous, not curated and episodic. What a dating application does with the same underlying psychology is expand the pool to a size the brain cannot process, strip the information down to a brief self-presentation the person has optimized for appeal, hand the result to an algorithm that ranks candidates by engagement metrics rather than compatibility, and then keep the user in a state of sustained comparison because sustainable comparison is the product's most valuable output. The anxiety of the irresolvable choice is not an unintended side effect. It generates platform opens.

There is a specific form of suffering in sustained optionality that I think doesn't get named enough. The feeling of having too many choices, not in the trivial sense of too many brands of cereal, but in the deeper sense of a life in which every commitment forecloses other commitments that are visible on a screen somewhere, where the person you chose is always adjacent in your awareness to the people you didn't choose and could still theoretically choose, where the relationship you're in is never quite sealed off from the market of relationships you could be in instead. The person sitting across from you at dinner is competing in your attention with everyone you've ever matched with, and neither of you chose this but both of you are living inside it.

The sperm count data from the 2023 meta-analysis update is worth sitting with for a moment because it's the kind of data that gets summarized and then filed away because there is nothing comfortable to do with it. A 62 percent decline in total sperm count among men in North America, Europe, and Australia between 1973 and 2018, with the rate of decline steepening measurably after 2000, and for the first time parallel declines documented across South America, Africa, and Asia. The lead author called it a canary in the coal mine, which is a phrase that means we should be watching this carefully, not a phrase that describes what we are doing. What we are doing is continuing the chemical exposures and stress conditions and dietary patterns that are most plausibly associated with the decline while conducting additional studies, because additional studies are what we do when we want to feel like we're doing something without changing anything.

The endocrine disruptors in consumer products, the phthalates in polyester, the BPA in thermal paper and food packaging, the pesticide residues in conventionally grown produce that show up in urine samples of people with no agricultural exposure whatsoever, these compounds don't arrive in the body separately and act separately. They arrive together and compound. The body has one endocrine system and it is running against all of them simultaneously, in a biological environment for which there is no evolutionary precedent, trying to maintain hormonal signaling in a chemical context that is undermining that signaling in ways that are individually subtle and collectively devastating. The research on each compound in isolation produces findings that industry can challenge. The research on what happens when they all land in the same body at once is far more difficult to fund and conduct.

I want to think about the word simultaneously. Every system I've been describing is operating simultaneously. The BPA is in my bloodstream at the same time the algorithm is feeding me threat signals at the same time the cortisol is suppressing my immune function at the same time the microplastics are in my tissue at the same time the PFAS compounds are wherever they are in my body at the same time the sleep deprivation is altering my prefrontal cortex at the same time I am trying to think clearly about all of this. These are not separate problems. They are one condition. The condition is me, or me is the condition, and I am sitting in a coffee shop trying to hold a cold cup and understand something that cannot be understood from inside itself.

Somewhere in this building right now there are people whose microbiome has been significantly altered by antibiotic exposure, whose gut sends signals their brains interpret as anxiety or depression, whose cortisol baseline is elevated from years of sleep disruption and information overload, whose inflammatory markers are higher than their grandparents' at the same age, who have never been told these things are connected. They've been told instead that they have several separate conditions, each with its own pharmaceutical treatment, and the treatment for each condition will modulate one variable in a system that is dysregulated across thirty variables simultaneously. The system is not broken. This is what it was designed to produce.

The barista near the espresso machine has been on shift since what I'm guessing was 6am based on when the shop opens. She's moving with the specific efficiency of someone who has mechanized her body to avoid spending energy on the parts of the job that don't require thought, and the parts that do require thought, talking to a person, reading what they want, she still does with genuine attention. She smiled at the child by the door in a way that wasn't a work smile. I noticed the difference. The work smile is an expression the muscles have memorized. The other one takes slightly longer to form and involves the muscles around the eyes. Paul Ekman spent thirty years mapping the difference. It's readable if you know what to look for, and I suspect she knows exactly which one she's deploying at any given moment and deploys them deliberately, which is its own form of labor that nobody is paying her for.

What she does for eight hours a day produces what economists call surplus value, the difference between what her labor generates and what she is paid for it, and that surplus is where profit comes from. This is not a political observation particularly. It's just how the system is built, and pointing it out has always been treated as if it were an accusation rather than a description. The actual problem is not that the mechanism exists but that the mechanism is never visible to the people inside it. She goes home at the end of her shift and buys things that the wages she earned by generating surplus pay for, and those things were produced by other people generating surplus elsewhere, and the system is coherent and largely invisible to everyone participating in it.

Her phone is behind the counter face-down and she looks at it between orders. The unremarkability of a given behavior is not evidence of its neutrality. It's evidence of how well it's been integrated. Slot machines were once remarkable. Cigarettes were once remarkable. The unremarkable is just what we call a remarkable thing that has been sufficiently normalized, made invisible by repetition, folded into the texture of daily life so thoroughly that questioning it feels strange, like questioning the sky.

I keep thinking about what Shoshana Zuboff described as the logic of the behavioral futures market, because I think that phrase is specific enough to be useful in a way that more general phrases are not. What Google discovered in 2001 was that the data produced by user interactions that wasn't necessary for running the service itself, the behavioral surplus, could be processed into probability estimates of future behavior and sold to advertisers who would pay handsomely for them. The product that changed the economic logic of the internet was not the search engine. It was the prediction. And a prediction is only valuable if it's accurate, and it's only accurate if the behavior it predicts can be influenced toward the predicted outcome, which means the business of selling predictions about behavior and the business of shaping behavior toward the predicted outcome are the same business. The communication platform is the interface that generates the behavioral data. The communication is incidental to the extraction.

Meta's advertising revenue for the fourth quarter of 2024 was $46.78 billion, which was 97 percent of the company's total income for that quarter. A company that derives 97 percent of its revenue from predicting and shaping user behavior for advertisers is not a communication platform in any meaningful functional sense. This is not an argument. It's arithmetic. The description changes what you're looking at. When you understand what the product is, you understand what you are, and what you are is the raw material.

The couple by the window is arguing now, quietly. I can tell from the quality of the silence between their sentences more than from the sentences themselves. The pauses have a different weight than they did an hour ago, something held in them rather than something resting. Whatever system they had for managing each other has run into something it wasn't designed for, or was designed for but can't hold right now, in this room, on this particular morning, with whatever they each brought to it. Watching people fail to reach each other in real time, even over something small, even when both of them clearly want to reach each other, is one of the more difficult things to sit across from. The distance between two people who are trying to close the distance is sometimes more heartbreaking than the distance between two people who have given up on closing it.

What I keep returning to is Robin Dunbar's finding about the upper functional limit of stable social groups in primates, which converges on around 150 people, the number at which you can meaningfully track individual relationships, shared histories, mutual debts, behavioral patterns, all the accumulated information that constitutes knowing someone in the full sense. In a group of that scale, social competition operated inside genuine interdependence. You competed with people you also needed. You would see the person you wronged tomorrow, and every day after. The social signal you emitted reached perhaps a few hundred meters before it dissipated into irrelevance.

Social media took that signal, distributed it globally, archived it permanently, indexed it for retrieval, attached a quantified feedback mechanism that assigns real numbers to social approval in real time, and then placed all of it inside recommendation systems optimized for engagement. The result is that the in-group and out-group circuitry that evolved to manage coalition loyalty in small groups of people who would share a fire together is now being activated at scale, continuously, against people a given person will never meet, in informational environments selected to keep the activation high enough to sustain attention. The performance of political identity that saturates social media is not politics in any historically recognizable sense. It's the body's coalition-management circuitry running without a coalition, against caricatures rather than people, in a medium that has a financial interest in keeping the threat signal strong enough that people keep coming back.

I have not been on my phone much this morning, which is unusual, and the not-being-on-it has created a specific quality of discomfort that I would call withdrawal if that word weren't attached to clinical connotations I don't want to claim. It's subtler than withdrawal. It's the feeling of a loop not completing, like a sentence that stops before its verb, a small persistent incompleteness that nags somewhere below the level of articulation. The loop that completes when I check the phone is not pleasure exactly, not in the way a good meal is pleasurable. It's the resolution of a tension that the device itself generates between checks. The reward doesn't need to be good. It needs to arrive unpredictably.

The cognitive effect of running that low-grade readiness for years, as part of the background state of daily life, is something the research is still working through because you can't fully study an effect while you're inside it, and all of us are inside it. What you can measure is that sustained stress hormone elevation, specifically the chronic cortisol exposure that results from maintaining a physiological readiness posture over years rather than hours, produces specific changes in hippocampal volume, in prefrontal cortex gray matter density, in amygdala reactivity. The brain changes. It responds to what you ask it to do and I have been asking mine to be vigilant and it has complied and the compliance has costs.

The hippocampus study Eleanor Maguire published in 2000 documented measurably greater gray matter density in London taxi drivers compared to controls, and the difference correlated with years of active navigation experience. The hippocampus grew in response to sustained navigational demand. The same principle operates in reverse. Any cognitive function that is consistently externalized, handed to a tool that performs it more efficiently, atrophies in the human at the rate of disuse. The neural tissue does not maintain itself against its own purposes. A generation that navigates primarily by GPS from early adulthood develops a different spatial cognitive architecture than a generation that navigated by memory and attention, and spatial cognition is not merely a practical skill. It's a cognitive architecture whose presence or absence shapes other functions including abstraction, working memory, and the capacity to hold complex information in mind without external scaffolding.

The broader implication, which nobody in the industry has any financial interest in examining, is that we are running a massive uncontrolled experiment on human cognitive architecture in real time, with no baseline, no control group, and no institutional body capable of assessing the results, because the institution that would be needed to assess them is itself embedded in the same technological environment. Every generation educates its children in the cognitive environment it finds. This is the first generation educating children in an environment whose cognitive demands are unlike any prior environment, and doing so without acknowledging that the demands are different or examining what that difference produces over a lifetime.

The child by the door has finished eating whatever she was eating and is now drawing something on a piece of paper with a red crayon. She draws with the same absolute concentration she ate with. Whatever she's making, it's serious to her, and the seriousness is uncomplicated by irony or self-consciousness. She glances up once, at nothing in particular, then back down. Her nervous system is not currently being managed by any algorithm. She is inside an experience without the meta-layer of monitoring that most adults have learned to maintain, that I am maintaining right now, that I have not been able to turn off for years. I notice I feel something watching her do this that I can't quite name, which is unusual because I have names for most of what I feel.

The urbanization that moved populations from small communities into cities over the past two centuries was not driven by the preferences of the people who moved. It was driven by the organizational requirements of an economy that needed concentrated labor near production facilities. The extended family and village networks that historically distributed childcare, eldercare, financial emergency absorption, food production, and psychological support across networks of twenty to forty people were incompatible with the kind of mobility that industrial capital required of its workforce. They were dissolved, not violently, not all at once, but through the slow pressure of economic incentives that made staying impossible for enough people that the networks unraveled. The nuclear household that replaced them was then asked to perform all of those functions within a private domestic space of two or three adults, which it was never capable of doing, and the excess that can't be absorbed generates market participation. Someone must pay, in money, for the childcare the village previously provided in obligation. Someone must pay for the eldercare the family previously absorbed. The therapy that addresses what the community previously held. The isolation is not a side effect. The isolated household whose every need must be individually purchased is the most economically productive unit of consumption that has ever existed.

The man near me has put his phone away, finally. I don't know why. Something shifted. He's looking at his partner now with a quality of attention that is different from the looking he was doing before. She hasn't noticed the shift yet, which is its own small thing, the way recovery from small disconnections is always slightly out of sync, the way you have to wait a little for the other person to catch up to where you've arrived. He reaches for his coffee. The cup is empty. He doesn't seem to notice that either.

The Surgeon General of the United States issued an advisory in May 2023 declaring loneliness a public health epidemic, which is a phrase that means something specific in medical terms. The mortality risk associated with chronic loneliness, pooled across multiple longitudinal studies, is equivalent to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day. The cardiovascular data is specific: a 29 percent increased probability of heart disease, 32 percent increased probability of stroke. The United Kingdom established a ministerial position for loneliness in 2018 because the health consequences had become measurable at a scale that justified policy response. The World Health Organization launched a commission on social connection in late 2023. None of this appears in the anthropological records of societies with dense physical community and intergenerational proximity at anything close to contemporary rates. The loneliness is not human nature. It is a product of particular arrangements.

Hyperindividualism is the philosophical framework that turned those arrangements into a moral claim. The idea that the individual is the primary social unit, that needing others is a weakness or failure of self-sufficiency, that dependence is a character flaw, was politically very useful to a system that required isolated consumers whose needs could be individually monetized. It is also physiologically nonsensical. Human beings are obligately social, which means their nervous systems require embeddedness in a group for normal function the way their bodies require food for normal function. The psychological research on social pain, the work showing that social exclusion activates the same neural circuitry as physical pain, is not metaphor. The circuits are the same circuits. The philosophy underneath hyperindividualism is not ancient. It's recent and specific. I had a conversation once with someone who studied mathematical intuition, specifically the question of how mathematicians arrive at conjectures before they have proofs. The phenomenologist Maurice Merleau-Ponty spent his career trying to describe the relationship between the perceiving body and the perceived world, arguing that perception is not a mental act applied to sensory data but something that happens in the body itself, that the body is not an instrument of perception but the subject of perception, that seeing is a bodily act in the same way that reaching or walking are bodily acts. There is a specific quality of contemporary suffering that I am trying to name and keep circling. It is the quality of being too aware. I want to think about what happens in the body when it is deprived of the specific kind of contact it needs. The intimacy researcher John Gottman, after thirty years of filming couples and following them longitudinally, found that the couples who stayed together and were happy shared a specific quality of interaction around what he called bids for connection. The response to a bid for connection, the choice to turn toward the bid or turn away from it, predicted the quality and longevity of the relationship more reliably than almost any other variable. The bids are small. They are almost invisible. The family structure that is supposed to contain all of this, two adults and their children in a private household, is an extremely recent invention and is performing extremely poorly by almost every measure except its usefulness as a unit of consumption. The cultural transmission question is worth staying with because it gets at something that the current moment has disrupted more profoundly than most people have noticed. Prior to mass media and then digital media, culture transmitted primarily through face-to-face interaction, story, ritual, and the accumulated practice of communities. What this produced was local variation, the specific character that a particular community of particular people in a particular place developed over time, and that variation was the medium through which people understood themselves as embedded in something specific and located rather than interchangeable and abstract. What mass media and then digital media produced was homogenization: the replacement of local cultural production with globally distributed content, which optimized for the widest possible audience rather than the deepest possible resonance with any particular community.

The belonging crisis is not experienced as philosophical. It's experienced as a specific recurring quality of social interaction, the sense that the interaction is happening but not landing, that you're present but not received, that the distance between you and the other person doesn't close no matter how much time you spend in proximity. This is the texture of the loneliness epidemic. Not the absence of people but the presence of people across whom genuine contact fails to happen, repeatedly, in a way that eventually stops feeling like failure and starts feeling like just how things are.

The tech industry's response to loneliness has been to produce connection platforms, which is the same impulse that produced the notification architecture and the variable ratio reinforcement schedule and the behavioral data extraction, applied to the specific problem of people not feeling connected enough. The tobacco playbook, as it's become known in public health literature, is the original documented model for manufacturing uncertainty around established findings. The equifinality problem in complex systems theory is that systems tend to arrive at the same stable state via multiple different pathways. Applied to social systems, it means that the arrangement of incentives and forces that produces the current set of outcomes tends to regenerate itself even when individual components change, because the incentive that produced the components also shapes their replacements. You can replace a captured regulatory official and the replacement gets captured by the same mechanisms that captured their predecessor. You can elect a reform candidate and the reform candidate operates within an institutional environment that shapes what reform is possible, which is typically less than what was promised, which is why reform candidates tend to disappoint, not because politicians are uniformly corrupt but because the institutional environment exerts continuous pressure in certain directions, and opposing that pressure requires sustained organized collective effort that the conditions of contemporary life are precisely arranged to make difficult to sustain.

The deep ecology tradition proposed that the root of ecological crisis was a philosophical error, the idea of the self as bounded and separate from its environment rather than constituted by relationship with it. The philosophical error is true. The pre-industrial populations that anthropologists describe as having an embedded sense of self, a sense of self as continuous with its context rather than prior to it, did not develop that through individual philosophical cultivation. The cultural transmission question is worth staying with because the current moment has disrupted it more profoundly than most people have noticed. Prior to mass media and then digital media, culture transmitted primarily through face-to-face interaction, story, ritual, and the accumulated practice of communities. What this produced was local variation, the specific character that a particular community developed over time, and that variation was the medium through which people understood themselves as embedded in something specific and located rather than interchangeable and abstract. What mass media and then digital media produced was homogenization.

And yet the homogenization is not uniform. What it produces is not a single global culture but the simultaneous proliferation of niche subcultures that exist online, each with its own vocabulary, references, and internal social relations, and no shared public culture that contains and connects them. The fragmentation produces people who are deeply embedded in their specific online communities and culturally invisible outside them, who share references and values with people they've never met across the world while being culturally incomprehensible to their geographic neighbors. The old question of belonging, of being a member of a place and a people, has been replaced by the new question of which community you can find online that will recognize you, and the answer to the new question does not produce the same thing as the answer to the old one.

The systems theory literature has a concept called leverage points, from the work of Donella Meadows, the systems thinker who wrote the most lucid book I've ever read about how complex systems work. A leverage point is a place in a system where a small change can produce large effects. The least effective leverage points are the ones people usually focus on, numbers and flows, the size of this policy or the rate of that process. The most effective leverage points are the ones almost nobody talks about, the goals of the system, the rules of the system, the power to change the rules of the system, the information flows in the system, and most powerfully, the mindset or paradigm out of which the system arises. The paradigm is the source of the system. Change the paradigm and everything downstream changes. But paradigms are the hardest things to change, because they are so deeply embedded that they are invisible, because they look like reality rather than a way of interpreting reality, because questioning them requires questioning everything you know.

The mindset or paradigm out of which the current system arises includes the idea that growth is progress, that individual preference is the highest value, that market mechanisms are the best way to allocate resources, that the natural world is a resource rather than a context, that the self is a bounded individual rather than a node in a web of relationships, and that the purpose of a human life is the accumulation of experiences and objects and outcomes rather than the depth and quality of presence to the life you're living. These are not facts. They are a paradigm. They were different at other times in other places and they could be different again.

I am not optimistic about the timescale on which the paradigm changes, because the timescale on which paradigms change is typically longer than the timescale on which the consequences of the current paradigm will arrive. The aquifer will be depleted before the paradigm that supports the agricultural system depleting it has shifted. I want to think about what it means to be fully present to a situation that you cannot change.
