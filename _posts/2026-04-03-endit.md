Being around people costs me something I can't name precisely and can't recover from the same night. I've been sitting with this for years, trying to decide if the damage is mine, and I keep arriving somewhere uncomfortable: I can feel the mechanics of it. Actual observable pattern. Who laughs and when. Who shifts their body toward whom. Who straightens when a more attractive or more powerful person enters the room, and who doesn't even register that a shift happened.
 
There's a man and a woman at a table near me right now. He said something a few minutes ago and she laughed, and I watched him take in the laugh. A small settling through his shoulders, some decision made without his awareness. She watched him take it in. And this is what gets called intimacy. I'm sitting here trying to work out what it would feel like to receive that exchange as nourishment, to sit inside it without counting anything. I've been trying to figure that out for most of my life. The honest possibility I keep arriving at is that I'm either missing something they have, or I have something they're missing, and both options are equally lonely.
 
The phone on the table in front of me has lit up six times in the past hour. I haven't touched it. But I counted. I noticed I was counting, which says something, though I'm not certain what. What I know is that each time it lights up there's a small physiological response, not quite excitement, something adjacent to dread. The same frequency as waiting for a result you didn't fully ask for. A researcher would call this variable-ratio reinforcement and would be describing the exact mechanism that makes slot machines work the way they do. I didn't work this out myself in a café. I read it in a paper about platform design, years ago. What stayed with me was a single word: engineered. Not grew. Not emerged. Engineered. Someone in a room decided on the reward interval, tested it, optimized it against behavioral data, and deployed it to a billion people without telling them what had been done to the interaction. The people running those optimization loops understood the neuroscience of compulsive behavior because they'd studied it. They applied it. They didn't explain it.
 
I put the phone face-down and leave it there. The woman laughs again at something else. The building hums.
 
What's in the air conditioning is something I think about more than is probably comfortable to admit. This particular building has that sealed-window smell, the kind where you know the air has been cycling through the same ductwork for years, past whatever filter they change on whatever schedule the lease requires, over the mold they probably don't know is there. I'm wearing a shirt I bought six months ago. Polyester. Twelve dollars, which means it was produced somewhere wages are low enough that twelve dollars leaves a margin, and the polyester itself is a petroleum derivative, and when you wash polyester it sheds somewhere around 700,000 synthetic microfibers per wash cycle in studies that have been replicated across different labs. Those fibers are now in municipal water systems, in fish tissue, and in 2022 a peer-reviewed study of twenty-two adult blood donors found microplastics in the blood of every single one of them. Not most. All twenty-two. What the fibers do once they're embedded in tissue is still being worked out because the studies take years and the polyester entered mass consumer goods before anyone started asking that question. The research is always chasing the exposure by a decade or two. That gap is not accidental. It's built into how risk is managed.
 
I pull the collar away from my neck slightly. It doesn't help.
 
The food I ate before coming here was wrapped in plastic. Before I ate it, it sat in a refrigerator cooled by a compressor that uses refrigerants that were phased out of consumer products in the US while being quietly increased in industrial cooling systems. The receipt I didn't take was coated in bisphenol-A, which transfers from the receipt paper to the skin at measurable concentrations within a few seconds of contact. BPA is an endocrine disruptor, meaning it mimics estrogen in the body at doses far below what regulatory agencies still consider acceptable. Male sperm counts in Western countries dropped approximately 59 percent between 1973 and 2011, according to a meta-analysis that pooled data from nearly 43,000 men. The researchers didn't identify a single cause. They identified a category: environmental exposures. The phthalates in my shirt are also endocrine disruptors. The pesticide residue on produce is measurable in urine within hours of consumption. The fluoride in municipal water, which is added specifically to affect biological tissue, is the only pharmaceutical deliberately mass-administered to a population without individual consent or dosage control. None of these facts are disputed in the scientific literature. The dispute is only about what level of exposure causes harm, a question that takes decades to answer definitively and which benefits from delay.
 
Outside the window the cell towers are visible. About thirty meters away from where I'm sitting there's a tower servicing this block. When the peer-reviewed literature on proximity to cell towers and cancer rates is pooled across independent studies, the signal is consistent enough that the International Agency for Research on Cancer placed radiofrequency electromagnetic fields in its Group 2B classification in 2011. Group 2B is the same classification as lead. The same as DDT. This is not a fringe organization issuing a fringe opinion. It's the cancer research arm of the World Health Organization making a formal classification that has regulatory implications. The regulatory agencies that set exposure limits for cell tower proximity haven't substantially updated those limits since the early 1990s, when the technology was different, the density of towers was far lower, and the research base was orders of magnitude smaller. The people who have updated the limits since are the industry bodies, using industry-funded studies. The mechanism for this - producing doubt as a product, funding research designed to find nothing, delaying regulatory action by a generation - was worked out and documented in the tobacco industry's internal records when those records became public through litigation. The tobacco companies knew their product caused cancer from the 1950s. The strategy to manage that knowledge ran for decades. When the documents finally surfaced, the mechanism was so clear that other industries adopted it explicitly as a model. This is in the court records.
 
I look at my phone sitting face-down and feel the radiation passing through the table and through my hand.
 
The woman at the nearby table is on her phone now too. Something must have required checking. What strikes me, watching, is that she doesn't look up after she puts it down. Whatever she found reorganized her attention slightly, left her somewhere between the table and wherever the notification came from. The man doesn't seem to notice. Or he notices and doesn't mention it because mentioning it would cost him something socially. This is what I mean when I say I can feel the mechanics. Not that the mechanics are all there is. I genuinely believe something real passes between people sometimes. But the mechanics are also there, and they're operating, and the platforms these two people are using were designed by teams of behavioral scientists who studied those mechanics and figured out how to insert themselves into the middle of them.
 
What this does to sleep is something I've spent time trying to understand because my sleep has been broken for years and I wanted to know why before I decided it was purely personal failure. The research on artificial light and circadian rhythm is not contested. The human body regulates its sleep-wake cycle through a light-sensing system that evolved under conditions where the light spectrum available after sunset was firelight and moonlight, and the cues for the body to produce melatonin were consistent for hundreds of thousands of years. Artificial light, particularly the blue-spectrum light from screens, suppresses melatonin production in a way that shifts the body's internal clock forward by hours. People who use screens until they sleep are running their bodies in a state of perpetual mild jet lag in the direction of east-to-west travel. The cardiovascular, metabolic, and immune consequences of chronic sleep disruption are documented across enough studies that the CDC lists insufficient sleep as a public health epidemic. An epidemic is a strong word for a regulatory agency to use. They used it.
 
Before artificial light, human sleep in non-industrial contexts appears to have been biphasic: a first sleep, a period of waking in the middle of the night that was used for reflection, prayer, sex, or conversation, and a second sleep. This pattern shows up in historical texts, in preindustrial diary records, in studies of contemporary populations without artificial light, and in sleep research where subjects are kept in darkness for extended periods. Their sleep spontaneously reorganizes into this pattern. What we call insomnia, for many people, is the first sleep working correctly while the body waits for a second sleep that the schedule of a working day makes impossible. We pathologize the body's original rhythm and treat it as a disorder to be corrected, ideally with a pharmaceutical.
 
The pharmaceutical for sleep disruption suppresses REM sleep. The pharmaceutical for the anxiety that partly comes from not sleeping properly also suppresses REM sleep. REM sleep is when the brain consolidates emotional memory and processes threat experiences. People who are chronically REM-deprived are more emotionally reactive, less capable of distinguishing genuine from false threats, and more susceptible to anxiety disorders. This is the condition that most people I know are managing on a daily basis. The condition is called normal.
 
What our nervous systems were actually built for, in the evolutionary sense, is worth thinking about clearly because the gap between that and the current situation is larger than I usually let myself sit with for long. The social unit that shaped human psychology was a band of roughly twenty-five to fifty people, mobile, with high mutual knowledge, shared food, shared child-rearing, and social accountability that couldn't be escaped because you lived in physical proximity to the same people across your entire life. Status competition was real but it was embedded in direct physical relationships where it was also real that you needed the person you were competing with, because you might need them to help carry something tomorrow. The emotional system that evolved in that context was calibrated for that scale of social complexity, that density of relationship, that level of physical danger, and that level of physical work. The current social environment runs that emotional system in conditions it was not calibrated for. Three hundred million people accessible through a screen. No physical accountability. Status competition in a population so large that the comparison class includes people you will never meet. Work that is sedentary and abstract in a body built for physical exertion. And the physical exertion, when it happens at all, happens in a gym, meaning in a controlled environment designed to simulate the physical demands that used to be inseparable from survival, now decoupled from any actual purpose beyond the body's maintenance.
 
I'm aware that framing this as mismatch can sound like an argument for returning to something. I'm not making that argument. You can't unknow what's been known, and I have no desire for a life where a bad winter kills half the people I love. What I'm describing is a diagnostic, not a prescription. The nervous system is running software in conditions that break it. The conditions were designed by people who had other priorities. Understanding this doesn't fix it. But not understanding it means blaming the software when the problem is the conditions.
 
The loneliness data, when I first found it, made me feel something between relief and horror. Relief because it meant the feeling wasn't purely personal failure. Horror because of what it implied about the scale of the thing. American adults, surveyed in 2020 by Cigna, reported that more than three in five felt lonely. In the UK the government appointed a Minister for Loneliness in 2018 because the health consequences of chronic social isolation were significant enough to require a dedicated policy response. The health consequences are cardiovascular, immunological, and neurological. The increase in mortality risk from chronic loneliness is comparable to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day, according to research that pooled data from multiple longitudinal studies. None of this was true to this degree in societies with denser physical community structures. The transition from that to this happened within a few generations and corresponded precisely with industrialization, urbanization, the breakdown of extended family structures, the privatization of domestic life into nuclear units, and finally digital life, which gave people the simulation of connection at the cost of the real thing. Each transition was framed as progress. Each step involved real gains. What wasn't announced was what was being traded away, because the people making those decisions weren't thinking about it, and the people who might have resisted weren't organized or powerful enough to do so.
 
When I read about what a physically stressed organism does differently than a chronically comfortable one, a piece of something I've been carrying for years shifted slightly into place. There's a body of research on what happens to biological and social systems when you remove the stressors that they evolved with. Immune systems that develop without exposure to pathogens and parasites develop inflammatory disorders at higher rates than immune systems that had those exposures in childhood. This is documented in industrialized populations, which have higher rates of autoimmune disease, allergies, and inflammatory conditions than comparable populations without the same level of early-life hygiene. Bones that are never stressed don't develop properly. Muscles that are never loaded atrophy. Social institutions that are insulated from volatility accumulate hidden fragility that doesn't reveal itself until a shock arrives, at which point they collapse in a way they wouldn't have if they'd been adapting to smaller shocks all along. A system that has been overprotected from the things that normally stress it is a system that has lost its adaptive capacity. When the thing it can't handle arrives, there's no reserve to draw on. This is what I watch happening in the people around me. Not because they're weak. Because the conditions that would have developed the relevant capacities were systematically removed from their experience as children and then from their adult lives, while the perception was maintained that this removal was care.
 
There's a man nearby checking his phone again. His jaw is slightly clenched. He doesn't know his jaw is clenched. I notice mine is too.
 
Who built these conditions. That's the question I've spent years trying to answer with more precision than "capitalism" or "modernity," because those are real answers that are also not specific enough to do anything with. The more specific version starts with the observation that every major decision about how modern mass society is organized - what gets in the food supply, what the environmental regulations look like, what gets taught in schools, what the foreign policy is, what the economic framework treats as a given - was made by specific people in specific rooms. And the people in those rooms were not randomly selected. They knew each other. Their children went to the same schools. They sat on each other's boards. They attended the same annual meetings. And the meetings were private.
 
The Council on Foreign Relations has been publicly visible since its founding in 1921. Its membership has included essentially every US Secretary of State in the modern era, the directors of major intelligence agencies, the editors and executives of major newspapers and television networks, the chairs of the largest financial institutions, and the presidents of the most influential universities. Its journal, Foreign Affairs, functions as the publication where policy gets announced before it becomes policy. The organization's public face is one of sophisticated analysis and internationalism. Its critics, including Carroll Quigley, a Georgetown historian who was given access to the internal records of its predecessor organizations, described it as a coordination mechanism for a network of people who intended to shape Anglo-American foreign policy across administrations regardless of electoral outcomes. Quigley's description of this network ran to more than a thousand pages of documented history. He was not sympathetic to it ideologically, but he didn't condemn it either. He thought coordination among intelligent people was preferable to incoherence. The book was published in 1966, went out of print, and was suppressed by the publisher - an event Quigley found baffling given that he'd written what he considered a scholarly history, not a polemic. Bill Clinton cited Quigley by name in a campaign speech as one of his intellectual influences. Quigley had been Clinton's professor at Georgetown.
 
The Trilateral Commission was founded in 1973 by David Rockefeller and Zbigniew Brzezinski, and its founding purpose is not secret. The founding documents describe a concern that democratic governance was becoming "ungovernable," that too much public participation in policy was creating instability, and that the elite coordination that had previously been managed within the US and UK needed to be extended to include Japan and Western Europe to manage the emerging post-Vietnam international order. The word "ungovernable" was used in a specific technical sense meaning: populations were demanding things that the policy framework wasn't designed to deliver, and this was inconvenient. The solution was technocratic: decisions that had political implications should increasingly be made by experts and implemented through institutions that were insulated from electoral pressure.
 
Every year since 1954, a meeting called the Bilderberg Conference has taken place. It draws together roughly 150 people from European and North American political leadership, finance, intelligence, media, and technology. The attendance list has been documented by journalists over decades. The meeting is formally private: no minutes are published, no transcripts released, no press access permitted. Journalists who have attempted to attend have been removed by private security. The people who attend deny that decisions are made at the meeting. They describe it as an informal discussion. What is peculiar about this description is that the people attending are precisely the people who make decisions in their professional lives, and a conversation between a prime minister, a central bank governor, the editor of a major newspaper, and the chief executive of a defense contractor about the current state of the world is not an informal discussion in any meaningful sense. What they decide together without accountability is different from what they decide separately with accountability, because the accountability structures don't reach the room.
 
The Club of Rome, which published "The Limits to Growth" in 1972, was a private group of scientists, businessmen, and former civil servants who had decided, on their own authority, to commission and publish research on the long-term consequences of industrial growth. The modeling in that report has held up reasonably well against subsequent decades of data. But the prescriptive conclusions that emerged from the Club's subsequent publications included repeated concern about global population and the need to manage it. The people at the Club of Rome had no democratic mandate to frame population as a problem requiring management. They were private individuals, self-selected, deciding among themselves that certain aspects of human behavior constituted a problem they were positioned to address.
 
Cecil Rhodes, who built a substantial fortune from diamond and gold extraction in Southern Africa using labor under conditions that would now be classified as exploitation, wrote in private correspondence and in his will about a goal of extending British imperial culture across the entire globe, bringing the United States back under British influence, and establishing a network of specially educated people who would carry those values into positions of power in whatever country they lived in. The Rhodes Scholarship, which produces alumni including multiple heads of state, senators, supreme court justices, intelligence directors, and media executives, is the institutional expression of this goal. The Round Table groups that grew out of Rhodes' network were documented by Quigley as the organizational ancestor of the CFR, the Royal Institute of International Affairs, and related bodies in other countries. The people in these institutions would not describe themselves as pursuing Rhodes' imperial vision. But the network he built, and the selection criteria for the network he built, produced a particular kind of person: cosmopolitan, elite-educated, instinctively internationalist, and accustomed to thinking of themselves as positioned to decide on behalf of populations they were not accountable to.
 
This is the coordination layer. The people who attend these meetings and belong to these institutions are not a single conspiracy with a unified plan. They disagree with each other on many things. What they share is a set of relationships, a set of assumptions about who has the right to make decisions, and an insulation from the consequences of those decisions. The factory worker who breathes synthetic fibers for thirty years is not at the Bilderberg Conference. The regulation that would have required different manufacturing methods was not passed because the people who could have pushed for it were in the same social and professional world as the people who funded the research casting doubt on the need for it. The gap is not conspiracy in the dramatic sense. It's structural conflict of interest, organized at a scale that makes it look like the natural order of things.
 
What runs deeper than these modern coordination structures, if you trace the lineages far enough, is something that secular political analysis tends to step around because it sounds like a different category of problem. The structures I've described have predecessors that are much older, and those predecessors understood themselves not primarily as political organizations but as custodians of knowledge that was too dangerous or too powerful to be made generally available. Whether or not you believe that framework, the framework shaped how those organizations understood their purpose, and it shapes the organizations that descended from them.
 
The Vatican has been continuously operating as a power structure for approximately fifteen hundred years. What intelligence capability looks like after fifteen hundred years of practice, maintained by an institution that operates in every country on earth, has diplomatic relations with almost every government, runs educational institutions at every level, hears confession from a significant fraction of political leaders in the Catholic world, and has permanent observer status in international bodies - I don't think that capability has been accurately described anywhere. The Jesuit Order, founded in 1540 specifically as an educated, mobile, disciplined response to the challenges facing the Church, was suppressed by papal order in 1773 because multiple European governments had petitioned the Vatican to remove it, citing its interference in domestic politics and intelligence activities. The suppression lasted forty-one years. During that period the order continued operating in Russia, where Catherine the Great refused to implement the suppression. When the order was reinstated in 1814 it had survived the gap by maintaining continuous operation in the one territory that allowed it. The structure of the order, which requires absolute obedience to the Superior General in ways that supersede even the Pope in daily operations, created an entity capable of acting with singular purpose across national boundaries in a way that no nation-state could, because no nation-state's authority reached all the territories where the order operated.
 
Opus Dei was founded in 1928. Its members in governments, universities, media organizations, and financial institutions across multiple countries are documented. Former members who have spoken publicly about internal practices describe an organization whose relationship with its members crosses the line between religious community and high-control group, in which members' professional decisions, living arrangements, and personal relationships are subject to organizational oversight, and in which leaving is made psychologically and socially costly. The organization has considerable influence in Spanish and Latin American political institutions, has members in the Vatican curia, and maintains a network of educational institutions that produce alumni in significant positions. Its formal legal status as a Personal Prelature gives it a degree of independence from local church hierarchies that is unique in the structure of the Church.
 
The Knights of Malta, the Sovereign Military Order of Malta, is a curiosity that most political science ignores because it doesn't fit the normal categories. It is a sovereign subject of international law. It issues passports. It has diplomatic relations with more than a hundred countries. It has no territory. Its membership has included CIA directors, multiple European heads of state and foreign ministers, and figures from the leadership of major financial institutions. Its membership is by invitation. It operates charitable institutions in conflict zones. It does other things that are not publicly documented.
 
The Italian Masonic Lodge known as Propaganda Due, or P2, was discovered when its membership list fell into the hands of investigating magistrates in 1981 during an investigation into a different matter. What the list showed was that the lodge's membership included the heads of all three Italian intelligence services, dozens of military officers, multiple members of parliament, the editors of major newspapers, and Roberto Calvi, the chairman of Banco Ambrosiano. Banco Ambrosiano had substantial financial connections to the Vatican Bank, including a letter from the Vatican Bank that functioned as a guarantee for loans that the Vatican Bank then denied issuing. Banco Ambrosiano collapsed in 1982. Roberto Calvi, nicknamed "God's Banker," was found hanging from scaffolding under Blackfriars Bridge in London that same year. The initial verdict was suicide. The case was reopened multiple times. An Italian court determined in a later proceeding that Calvi had been murdered. The people convicted of ordering the murder were connected to the Sicilian mafia. The connection between organized crime, Freemasonry, political intelligence services, and the Vatican Bank's financial operations was documented in judicial proceedings and not seriously contested. What it meant at a strategic level - who was coordinating what, for what purpose - remained unresolved.
 
The families whose wealth and title trace back furthest in European history, the ones whose names appear on palaces and in papal records going back to the medieval period - the Colonna, the Massimo, the Aldobrandini, the Medici, the Bourbon branches that survived the revolutions, the Habsburg lines - have a continuity of social position that is difficult to describe accurately in modern political terms because we don't have a frame for the kind of power that outlasts every political system around it. These families have been present at every major reorganization of European power for a thousand years. They survived the Roman collapse, the medieval church, the Reformation, the French Revolution, the world wars. The mechanisms by which this survival was accomplished included intermarriage, conversion when necessary, strategic alliances with whatever rising power needed legitimizing, and the maintenance of financial and social positions that made them useful to whatever structure was ascendant. Whether they exercise coordinated power today in any organized sense I don't know. What I know is that the families exist, their networks are real, and the assumption that a thousand years of accumulated relationship and influence simply dissipated when modern democratic governance arrived is an assumption that requires justification it hasn't received.
 
Freemasonry, in its modern institutional form, traces its lineage to the early 18th century in England, though it claims a much older symbolic heritage. The structure of the organization - progressive degrees of initiation, each revealing more of the organization's symbolic and philosophical substance, with full understanding reserved for the highest levels - is an organizational structure with a particular purpose: it allows an organization to appear to have many members while actually concentrating meaningful participation in a much smaller group. A Mason at the third degree of the Blue Lodge is technically a full member. A Mason at the 32nd degree of the Scottish Rite or the 90th of the Memphis-Misraim Rite knows things that the third-degree Mason does not, and the two are technically members of the same organization. The Scottish Rite was formalized in Charleston, South Carolina in 1801. The Memphis Rite has roots that Masonic historians trace to Egypt, through various intermediaries. What the higher degrees teach philosophically is drawn from Hermeticism, Kabbalah, Neoplatonism, and the Rosicrucian tradition. The Rosicrucians, who appeared publicly in early 17th-century Germany through a series of manifestos, claimed to represent a secret society founded in the medieval period that had preserved a body of knowledge combining Christian theology with Hermetic philosophy and alchemical symbolism. Whether the historical order they claimed to represent existed or whether the manifestos were a thought experiment is debated by scholars. What is not debated is that the manifestos catalyzed the formation of actual organizations, including eventually the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn in 1887, which produced an enormous influence on 20th-century Western occultism, including the work of Aleister Crowley and the Theosophical Society, which in turn influenced Helena Blavatsky's system, which in turn influenced figures in the early 20th century who shaped education, psychology, and cultural institutions in ways that are still active.
 
Skull and Bones at Yale was founded in 1832. Its membership, partially documented through leaks and research, includes figures from the Bush family across multiple generations, John Kerry, William Howard Taft, and a disproportionate number of people who ended up leading the CIA, the State Department, and major financial institutions. The organization's initiation rituals, described by members who have spoken publicly, involve the kind of humiliation and disclosure of personal secrets that function, in a high-control group context, as bonding mechanisms that make leaving psychologically costly and create mutual vulnerability between members. An organization that knows intimate personal information about all its members has leverage over those members in ways that conventional professional relationships don't. The relationship between Yale's secret societies and the CIA was direct enough in the post-World War II period that the organization was sometimes described, by people who should know, as a graduate program.
 
Now I want to say something about what runs underneath all of this, because I think the political and organizational layer is real but it isn't the bottom layer, and not acknowledging the bottom layer produces an analysis that misses something important. The bottom layer is religious, or was religious, or uses religious language to describe something that is at minimum a coherent metaphysical framework and at maximum a theological reality that I don't have the equipment to adjudicate.
 
The mystery schools of antiquity - the Eleusinian mysteries, the Mithraic mysteries, the mysteries of Isis and Osiris, the Pythagorean brotherhood - were not public religions. They were initiatory traditions in which participation required formal application, testing, and progressive stages of revelation. What the Eleusinian initiates were shown at the highest level of initiation, in the telesterion at Eleusis, is not precisely known because the oath of secrecy held across two thousand years of continuous operation. What is documented is that initiates described the experience as transformative, that it involved altered states, that it was understood as a death and rebirth, and that access to the full content of the tradition was the reward for surviving successive initiations. The Mithraic mysteries operated in underground chambers called mithraea, their membership was exclusively male, and the imagery of the tradition involved a figure killing a bull in a posture that is replicated with remarkable consistency across dozens of archaeological sites, though the mythology that gives it meaning was apparently never written down and has to be reconstructed from the images themselves. Pythagoras founded a community in which members were required to keep the mathematical discoveries of the group secret from non-members, in which there were degrees of initiation, and in which the philosophical system included elements that Pythagorean scholars have described as a synthesis of Greek and Egyptian and Babylonian traditions. The connection between the mathematical understanding preserved in the Pythagorean tradition and the architectural encoding of mathematical ratios in sacred buildings across multiple ancient cultures is one of the more interesting threads running through the history of esoteric knowledge transmission.
 
The symbolic content of these traditions shares a recurring structure: a cosmos understood as a hierarchy of levels of reality, with ordinary material existence at the bottom and a transcendent principle at the top, with a series of intermediate levels governed by planetary or angelic intelligences. The Gnostic traditions that proliferated in the early centuries of the common era took this structure and added a darker element: the material world is not the creation of the highest principle but of a lower, imperfect, possibly malevolent being called the Demiurge, who created material existence as a prison for sparks of divine light that are the true nature of human consciousness. The Demiurge is not evil in the way a villain is evil. It is limited, and its limitation makes it controlling, because it doesn't know what it doesn't know. The human condition, in this framework, is one of divine consciousness imprisoned in matter, conditioned by the false reality of the Demiurge's creation to mistake that reality for the totality of what exists. The work of spiritual development is the recovery of the awareness of what you actually are underneath the conditioning.
 
I'm not advocating this cosmology. I'm noting that it is the cosmological framework that runs, in various forms, through Pythagoreanism, through Neoplatonism, through Hermeticism, through Kabbalah, through the Rosicrucian tradition, through Freemasonry at its higher degrees, through the Theosophical system, and through the esoteric currents that influenced the founding of organizations that went on to have significant cultural and political impact. Whether the people who hold this framework believe it literally or use it as a symbolic system for psychological and organizational purposes, the framework shapes what they think the purpose of human existence is, what they think ordinary people are, and what they think they are in relation to ordinary people. An organization that understands itself as preserving divine knowledge from the uninitiated masses has a fundamentally different relationship to the concept of public disclosure than an organization that simply has trade secrets.
 
Saturn appears in these traditions in a specific way that is worth noting carefully. In the Roman religious system, Saturn was the god of time, of agricultural cycles, of the social order, of the law, and in his darker aspect, of limitation and of sacrifice. The Saturnalia, the festival in his honor, involved a period of social inversion in which the normal order was temporarily reversed before being restored, reinforced. In the astrological tradition that informed both ancient religion and later Hermeticism, Saturn governs time, constraint, discipline, and karma, and is associated with the color black, with lead among the metals, with structures that persist through time, and with the harvest that requires death to produce life. The symbolic resonance of Saturn with institutions that use time as a tool of control - that accumulate power by outlasting the humans who challenge them, that harvest without appearing to plant - is not an accident of mythology. The people who built those institutions knew the mythology intimately.
 
The deity called Moloch in the Hebrew scriptures, Baal in the Phoenician tradition, Kronos in the Greek tradition, and Saturn in the Roman, is not precisely a single deity but a complex of overlapping worship practices across Semitic and Mediterranean cultures that shared the characteristic of requiring significant sacrifice, including in some documented contexts the sacrifice of children, in exchange for divine favor or protection of the community. The Tophet at Carthage is an archaeological site that contains the cremated remains of thousands of children in urns, alongside votive offerings, and has been interpreted by archaeologists as a site of sacrificial practice. The debate among scholars is about the precise nature of the practice, not about whether sacrifice happened. The Hebrew scriptures reference child sacrifice to Moloch as a practice that existed among the Israelites and was condemned by the prophets, which implies it was real enough to require condemnation.
 
The Bohemian Grove is a private encampment in the redwood forests of Northern California that hosts a gathering of approximately 2,500 men every summer. The membership includes former US presidents, current and former members of the cabinet, the leadership of major defense contractors, the executives of major financial institutions, and figures from the entertainment and media industries. The gathering is private, attended by invitation only, and surrounded by private security. What is documented about its activities includes the Cremation of Care ceremony, performed on the first night of the gathering before a large stone owl figure, in which an effigy representing worldly cares is ceremonially burned. The ceremony involves robes, torches, theatrical staging, and an explicitly ritual structure. This is not contested by the Grove's members, some of whom have written about it fondly as a tradition. What is described by insiders as a lighthearted tradition, when viewed from outside through the lens of the symbolic history I've described, has a different texture. The owl is a symbol associated with Minerva, with wisdom, and with night seeing, a recurring symbol in organizations that understand themselves as possessing knowledge unavailable to daylight consciousness. The burning of care - the deliberate symbolic abandonment of moral concern for the duration of the gathering - is either a harmless tradition or a ritual acknowledgment of what it costs to make the decisions these people make, conducted in the context of a symbolic tradition whose meaning they understand better than their public statements suggest.
 
The category of satanic ritual abuse became entangled with a panic in the 1980s that produced false accusations and destroyed innocent people's lives, and the backlash from that panic effectively made any discussion of the actual documented cases sound like a repetition of the panic. The Franklin case in Nebraska, involving a credit union manager named Larry King, produced FBI investigations, grand jury testimony, and a documentary that was scheduled to air on the Discovery Channel and was pulled hours before broadcast. The grand jury testimony, which is public record, contains descriptions of abuse by children who named political figures among their abusers. The investigation was not resolved in any way that produced accountability for those figures. The documentary, which was titled "Conspiracy of Silence," exists in incomplete form and has circulated through channels that the mainstream would classify as fringe precisely because the mainstream classification of the topic as fringe is what makes discussing it safely impossible. The language we use to describe this situation is itself managed. Words that became associated with fringe thinking or conspiracy theory - even when the underlying evidence is real - lose their utility as analytical tools because using them triggers a dismissal response before the substance can be engaged. "Conspiracy theory" was coined as a phrase in CIA document 1035-960, circulated in 1967 to field agents and media contacts, which outlined a strategy for managing public discussion of the Warren Commission report by associating skepticism of the report with psychological abnormality. The phrase existed before the document. The document operationalized it as a rhetorical weapon. This is not speculation. The document exists and is available through the National Security Archive. From that point forward, the phrase became increasingly useful as a way to end conversations about real documented coordination between powerful institutions.
 
The ecology I grew up inside, by which I mean the literal physical ecology of the planet, is measurably worse than the ecology my grandparents grew up inside. This is documented without controversy. The number of insects has declined by approximately 70 percent since 1970 in the areas with the best longitudinal data. Bird populations that depend on insects have declined proportionally. Soil organic matter in agricultural land has been depleted by industrial farming methods at a rate that has already caused yield decline in some regions. Freshwater systems contain pharmaceutical residue from medications that are not filtered by standard water treatment: antidepressants, contraceptive hormones, antibiotics. The antibiotics in the water supply are measurable. The hormonal compounds in the water supply are measurable. The fish in rivers downstream from water treatment plants show endocrine disruption at measurable rates. Sperm counts are declining. Children's cognitive development scores correlate negatively with levels of certain environmental chemical exposures at the population level. None of this is contested in the peer-reviewed literature. All of it is contested in the policy space, where the industries responsible for the chemicals have learned from the tobacco model how to manage the gap between the science and the regulation.
 
What has been lost in the severance from the natural world is harder to document because it's not the kind of thing that produces a measurable metric. When populations of hunter-gatherers have been studied - before contact, before disruption, in the documented records of anthropologists who reached them in those conditions - what appears consistently is a relationship with the living world that the industrial world doesn't have a word for. Not spirituality in the Western religious sense. Something more like perceptual participation. The world is not an object being viewed by a subject. The world is a field of relationships in which the human is one participant among others, all of which are in some sense alive and in some sense meaningful. This perception has been documented as producing genuine psychological coherence - the sense of being embedded in something that extends beyond the self, that provides both context and belonging. The pathological rates of anxiety, depression, meaninglessness, and loneliness in industrial populations don't appear in the anthropological record of those other populations at anything like the same prevalence. The variable that changed is not genetic. It's the relationship to the living world, the density of human community, and the embeddedness of daily life in cycles of natural time that the industrial mode severed.
